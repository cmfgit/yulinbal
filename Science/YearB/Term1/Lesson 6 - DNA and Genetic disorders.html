What Are Genetic Disorders?

Remember from previous lessons:

  • DNA is stored in chromosomes
  • Humans have 23 pairs of chromosomes (46 total)
  • Chromosomes carry genes that give instructions for making proteins
  • Proteins do all the work in our bodies

üîç Definition: Genetic Disorder

A genetic disorder is a health condition caused by changes (mutations) in genes or chromosomes.

These changes affect how proteins are made, which can cause problems in how the body works.

Slide 2 of 10

Two Types of Genetic Disorders

Type 1: Chromosome Disorders

‚Ä¢ Problems with whole chromosomes (too many, too few, or damaged)

‚Ä¢ Example: Having an extra chromosome 21

‚Ä¢ Affects many genes at once

Type 2: Single Gene Disorders

‚Ä¢ Problems with one specific gene on a chromosome

‚Ä¢ The gene has wrong instructions, so it makes the wrong protein

‚Ä¢ Examples: Problems with genes on chromosome 7 or chromosome 11

Important to Remember:

  • Genetic disorders are not contagious - you can't catch them
  • They are often inherited (passed from parents)
  • Some can be managed with treatment
  • People with genetic disorders can live full, meaningful lives

Down Syndrome - Part 1

üìç CHROMOSOME 21

What Causes It?

People with Down syndrome have an EXTRA copy of chromosome 21.

‚Ä¢ Normal: 2 copies of chromosome 21

‚Ä¢ Down syndrome: 3 copies of chromosome 21

‚Ä¢ This is called "Trisomy 21"

‚Ä¢ They have 47 chromosomes instead of 46

Why Does an Extra Chromosome Cause Problems?

The extra chromosome 21 means cells make TOO MUCH of certain proteins.

When there's too much of some proteins, it affects how the body and brain develop.

Cystic Fibrosis - Part 1

üìç CHROMOSOME 7

‚Ä¢ The gene is called the CFTR gene

‚Ä¢ The mutation means the gene gives WRONG instructions

‚Ä¢ This makes a faulty protein that doesn't work properly

‚Ä¢ Both parents must pass on a faulty gene for a child to have CF

The CFTR protein normally controls salt and water movement in cells.

When it doesn't work:

‚Üí Thick, sticky mucus builds up in organs

‚Üí Particularly affects lungs and digestive system

DNA ‚Üí RNA ‚Üí Protein Connection:

Cystic Fibrosis - Part 2

üìç CHROMOSOME 7

Sickle Cell Disease - Part 1

üìç CHROMOSOME 11

What Causes It?

A mutation in ONE gene on chromosome 11.

‚Ä¢ The gene makes hemoglobin (protein in red blood cells)

‚Ä¢ Just ONE letter change in the DNA causes the problem!

Hemoglobin normally carries oxygen in red blood cells.

‚Üí Red blood cells become stiff and curved (sickle-shaped)

‚Üí They get stuck in blood vessels

‚Üí They break apart easily

DNA ‚Üí RNA ‚Üí Protein Connection:

Chromosome 11 has ONE wrong DNA letter ‚Üí makes wrong RNA ‚Üí makes wrong hemoglobin protein ‚Üí protein changes red blood cell shape ‚Üí blood cells can't work properly

Sickle Cell Disease - Part 2

üìç CHROMOSOME 11

What Does It Affect?

Common Problems:
  • Pain crises: Sickle cells block blood flow, causing severe pain
  • Fatigue: Not enough healthy red blood cells to carry oxygen
  • Infections: Damaged spleen makes fighting infections harder
  • Organ damage: Blocked blood vessels can damage organs over time

Managing Sickle Cell Disease:

‚Ä¢ Pain medication during crises

‚Ä¢ Medication to reduce sickling

‚Ä¢ Blood transfusions when needed

‚Ä¢ Drinking lots of water helps prevent crises

‚Ä¢ Avoiding extreme temperatures and high altitudes

Interesting Fact:

Sickle cell disease is more common in people whose ancestors came from Africa, Mediterranean countries, Middle East, and India. The sickle cell gene provides some protection against malaria!

Key Takeaways

1. The DNA ‚Üí RNA ‚Üí Protein Connection

DNA has instructions ‚Üí RNA copies them ‚Üí Proteins do the work

When DNA is wrong, the proteins are wrong, and the body doesn't work properly

2. Chromosomes Matter

Each chromosome carries hundreds or thousands of genes

Problems with different chromosomes cause different disorders

Remember:

  • Genetic disorders are NOT the person's fault
  • They are NOT contagious
  • People with genetic disorders are valuable members of our community
  • Medical science is constantly finding better treatments
  • Many people with genetic disorders live full, meaningful lives
