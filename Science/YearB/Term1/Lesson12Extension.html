Background — Why Cattle Genetics?
Setting the scene for your research

Australia is one of the world's largest beef and dairy producers. The cattle industry contributes over $14 billion to the Australian economy every year. Behind every herd of cattle is a hidden world of genetics — mutations that have been selected, bred, and sometimes engineered to produce more meat, more milk, better disease resistance, and healthier animals.

Some of these mutations happened naturally over thousands of years of evolution. Others were selectively bred by farmers over generations. And some of the most recent changes have been made deliberately using genetic technology in a laboratory. In this task, you will investigate real mutations found in cattle — and decide whether they are helpful, harmful, neutral, or complicated.

You will also take a position on a key ethical question facing the modern cattle industry: should we be using genetic technology to deliberately change the DNA of cattle?

The Mutations — 5 Real Cases to Investigate
Read each case carefully. You will use these in your research questions.
Helpful ✓

Case 1 — Double Muscling (Myostatin Mutation)

Belgian Blue and Piedmontese cattle carry a mutation in the MSTN gene, which normally limits muscle growth. When this gene is mutated, the "stop growing" signal is broken — and the cattle develop enormous muscles, sometimes twice the normal size.

Result: Dramatically more beef per animal. High economic value for the beef industry.

Complication: The calves often cannot be born naturally and require caesarean sections. The mutation that benefits farmers causes significant health problems for the animals.

Harmful ✗

Case 2 — Spinal Muscular Atrophy (SMA)

Some breeds of cattle, particularly Brown Swiss, carry a recessive mutation that causes Spinal Muscular Atrophy — a condition where the muscles progressively weaken because the spinal cord cannot send proper signals. Calves are born appearing healthy but deteriorate rapidly.

Result: Affected calves cannot stand, feed, or survive. Death occurs within weeks.

Complication: Carrier animals (with one copy) are completely healthy — farmers may not know their herd carries the mutation until affected calves are born.

Helpful ✓

Case 3 — Polledness (Hornless Cattle)

Naturally horned cattle can injure each other and farm workers. A mutation in the POLLED locus on chromosome 1 causes cattle to be born without horns — called "polled" cattle. This mutation exists naturally in some breeds (like Angus) but not others (like Holsteins).

Result: Safer farms, reduced animal injuries, no need for painful dehorning procedures.

Complication: Scientists have now used CRISPR gene editing to insert the polled mutation into dairy breeds. This raises ethical questions about genetically modified food animals.

Complex ⚡

Case 4 — Coat Colour (MC1R Gene)

The MC1R gene controls coat colour in cattle. Different mutations produce black, red, or yellow coats. In some breeds, coat colour is linked to heat tolerance — lighter coats absorb less sunlight and keep animals cooler in hot climates like northern Australia.

Result: Appears neutral (just a colour change) but has real survival implications in hot climates.

Complication: Shows how a seemingly neutral mutation can become helpful or harmful depending on the environment — a key concept in natural selection.

Genetic Technology

Case 5 — Disease Resistance via CRISPR (Bovine Respiratory Disease)

Bovine Respiratory Disease (BRD) is the most costly disease in the cattle industry worldwide, killing millions of calves each year. Scientists in the USA and Australia have used CRISPR-Cas9 gene editing to introduce mutations that make cattle resistant to the viruses that cause BRD. Rather than waiting for a natural mutation to arise and spread through a population over thousands of years, scientists are deliberately writing it into the genome.

Result: Could save billions of dollars and millions of animal lives annually. Reduces the need for antibiotics in cattle farming — which also reduces antibiotic resistance risk for humans.

Complication: These are genetically modified organisms (GMOs). In Australia, GM food animals are regulated by the Office of the Gene Technology Regulator (OGTR). No GM cattle are currently approved for human consumption in Australia. Ethical questions remain about long-term ecological impacts.

Key Terms for This Task
Make sure you understand these before you begin
Mutation
A change in the DNA base sequence. Can be helpful, harmful, or neutral.
Selective Breeding
Choosing animals with desirable traits to breed together over many generations to make those traits more common.
Recessive Mutation
A mutation that only causes a noticeable effect when two copies are present (one from each parent). A carrier has one copy and appears healthy.
CRISPR-Cas9
A genetic technology that acts like "molecular scissors" — it can cut DNA at a specific location and insert, delete, or change a gene with great precision.
Genotype
The actual genes an organism carries in its DNA (e.g., one copy of a mutation vs two copies).
Phenotype
The observable traits produced by those genes — what you can actually see or measure in the animal.
GMO
Genetically Modified Organism — an organism whose DNA has been deliberately changed using laboratory technology, not just selective breeding.
Natural Selection
The process where mutations that help survival are passed on more often, and harmful mutations die out — over many generations this changes populations.

Research Questions
Answer all 6 questions using the case studies above and your own research
Question 1 — Classify
For each of the 5 cattle mutations, classify the mutation as helpful, harmful, neutral, or complex. Justify each classification with at least one piece of evidence from the case study.
💡 Hint: Remember that "helpful" or "harmful" can depend on who you're asking — the farmer, the animal, or the environment.
Question 2 — Explain the Science
The Double Muscling mutation (Case 1) involves a gene called MSTN. Explain in your own words how a single mutation in one gene can cause such a dramatic change in the phenotype of an animal. Use the terms gene, mutation, protein, and phenotype in your answer.
💡 Hint: Think about what a gene does — it codes for a protein. What happens when the protein doesn't work properly?
Question 3 — Genetics Concepts
Spinal Muscular Atrophy (Case 2) is caused by a recessive mutation. A farmer has a herd of Brown Swiss cattle. Two completely healthy-looking parents produce a calf with SMA. Using the terms carrier, recessive, genotype and phenotype, explain how this is possible.
💡 Hint: Draw a Punnett square if it helps. What must both parents' genotypes be?
Question 4 — Environment & Mutation
Case 4 shows that coat colour mutation might be neutral in one environment but helpful in another. Using this example, explain why scientists say "whether a mutation is helpful or harmful often depends on the environment." Can you think of another example — in any animal — where this is true?
💡 Hint: Think about camouflage, climate, or food availability in different environments.
Question 5 — Genetic Technology
Compare natural mutation and selective breeding with CRISPR gene editing. What is the key difference in how each one changes the DNA of cattle? Which method do you think is faster? Which do you think raises more ethical concerns? Explain your reasoning.
💡 Hint: Consider: how long does each method take? Who controls the outcome? Are there risks we don't know about yet?
Question 6 — Take a Position (Extended Response)
Australia currently does not allow genetically modified cattle to enter the food supply. Using evidence from at least THREE of the case studies above, write a structured argument either FOR or AGAINST allowing CRISPR-edited cattle to be used in Australian farming. Your response must include scientific evidence, at least one ethical consideration, and a clear conclusion.
💡 Hint: A strong argument considers both sides before taking a position. Aim for 200–300 words.
