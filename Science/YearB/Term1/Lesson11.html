🧬 Quick Recap
What's a Mutagen? 🤔
📖
Mutation
A change in the DNA code — like a typo in the instruction manual for your body.
⚡
Mutagen
Anything in the environment that causes or increases the chance of a mutation. Think of it as the thing that creates the typo.
🧫
Why Does It Matter?
If mutations happen in cells that control growth, they can lead to cancer — cells that copy themselves out of control.

💡 Analogy: Imagine your DNA is a book with millions of instructions. A mutagen is like someone sneaking in and changing or deleting random words. Sometimes nothing changes. Sometimes the whole meaning is ruined — and that can cause cancer.

☀️ Case Study 1 — UV Radiation Australia's #1 Skin Cancer Risk
☀️ Sunlight & DNA Damage
1
What happens: UV-B radiation from the sun hits skin cells. It causes two DNA bases (thymine) to stick together incorrectly — called a thymine dimer. This corrupts the instruction at that point.
2
Australia Case Study: Australia has one of the highest skin cancer rates in the world. Approximately 2 in 3 Australians will be diagnosed with skin cancer by age 70. Low-SES communities often work outdoors (construction, farming, cleaning) with less access to sun protection.
3
Connection to Mutations: UV damage builds up over years. When the mutation hits a gene that controls cell division (called a tumour suppressor gene), cells can multiply uncontrolled = melanoma (skin cancer).
4
Prevention: Sunscreen (SPF 50+), hats, shade, and avoiding peak UV hours (10am–3pm) all reduce DNA damage. Free sunscreen is available at many public spaces in NSW.
🚬 Case Study 2 — Tobacco Smoke 70+ Known Mutagens
🚬 Chemicals That Attack Your DNA
1
What happens: Tobacco smoke contains 70+ chemical mutagens (including benzene, formaldehyde, and polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons). These chemicals enter lung cells and directly bond to DNA bases — bending and breaking the code.
2
Real Case Study: Research shows smoking rates are significantly higher in low-SES communities due to stress, marketing targeting, and fewer cessation resources. Lung cancer is the leading cause of cancer death in Australia and disproportionately affects working-class communities.
3
Connection to Mutations: Tobacco mutagens cause specific mutations in the TP53 gene — the body's main "damage control" gene. When TP53 is mutated, damaged cells can't be stopped from dividing.
4
Important: Second-hand smoke also causes mutations. You don't have to smoke to have your DNA damaged — being around smokers regularly exposes your cells to the same chemicals.
🍺 Case Study 3 — Diet & Alcohol Everyday Choices Matter
🍟 What You Eat Can Change Your DNA
1
Alcohol: When your body breaks down alcohol, it produces acetaldehyde — a powerful chemical mutagen. Acetaldehyde directly damages DNA in liver, throat, and mouth cells. Regular heavy drinking dramatically raises cancer risk.
2
Processed meats & charred food: Contain chemicals called nitrosamines and heterocyclic amines (HCAs) — classified as probable carcinogens by the WHO. HCAs form when meat is cooked at high heat or burnt.
📊
Real Data (Australia)
Alcohol is linked to 6 types of cancer in Australia. Low-SES communities often have less access to fresh food and higher rates of alcohol use due to social stressors — increasing DNA mutation risk.

✅ Good news: Eating a diet rich in vegetables and fruit provides antioxidants that help repair DNA damage. Bulk vegetables, legumes and wholegrain bread are affordable options that reduce cancer risk.

🏭 Case Study 4 — Industrial & Environmental Chemicals
🏭 Chemicals in Our Neighbourhoods
1
Asbestos (Australia's biggest example): Used widely in Australian homes before 1987. Asbestos fibres lodge in lung cells and cause persistent DNA damage over decades, leading to mesothelioma (lung cancer). Workers in blue-collar industries were disproportionately exposed.
2
Benzene & Formaldehyde: Found in petrol fumes, cleaning products, and some building materials. Communities near busy roads or industrial areas (often lower-SES areas) have higher air pollution exposure — and higher rates of certain leukaemias and cancers.
3
Why does this affect low-SES communities more? Cheaper housing is often located near factories, main roads, and industrial sites. This is called environmental inequality — poorer communities bear more environmental health risks through no choice of their own.
4
What can individuals do? Ventilate homes, avoid burning rubbish, check for asbestos in older homes (free testing kits available in some NSW councils), and advocate for clean air in your community.

🧬 Going Deeper — DNA Repair Mechanisms
Your Body Fights Back! 🛡️
1
Base Excision Repair (BER): Special enzymes "patrol" your DNA. When they find a damaged base, they cut it out and replace it — like a spell-checker.
2
Nucleotide Excision Repair (NER): Specifically targets UV-damaged DNA (thymine dimers). Cuts out a larger section and fills it in correctly.
3
When repair fails: If the damage is too great — or if the repair enzymes are overwhelmed — the mutation is permanent. This is when cancer risk increases.
⚗️
Why repair fails with heavy mutagen exposure
Every cigarette, every severe sunburn, every heavy drinking session creates thousands of DNA errors. Your repair enzymes can handle normal background damage — but not years of heavy mutagen exposure.

⚠️ Key fact: Cancer is rarely caused by a single mutation. It usually takes multiple mutations in the same cell over time — which is why reducing mutagen exposure early matters.
